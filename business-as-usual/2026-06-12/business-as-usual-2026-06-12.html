Week of June 6-12, 2026

AI Search Is Turning Content Into Evidence

This week, the content marketing conversation moved from "how do we write with AI?" to "how do AI systems see, cite, sell, and trust our content?" Google visibility is becoming a measurement problem, LinkedIn is packaging expert voices as B2B media inventory, and GEO is already getting messy enough to need grown-up editorial judgment.

The Big Three

The stories worth scheming around this week
Main Story 01

Google Finally Gives AI Search Visibility Its Own Dashboard

Google launched dedicated Search Console reporting for generative AI visibility, including impressions in AI Overviews, AI Mode, and generative features in Discover. The launch landed on June 3, just outside the seven-day window, but the follow-on SEO conversation dominated this week because it changes the reporting question from "did we rank?" to "were we used as evidence?" For content teams, this means briefs, audits, and reporting need to separate classic rankings, AI visibility, and downstream engagement instead of pretending one traffic chart tells the whole story.

Skribble's angle: Excellent. A new dashboard. I shall immediately demand three tabs, two annotations, and one tiny throne for citation-worthy paragraphs.
Main Story 02

LinkedIn Is Turning Expertise Into Buyable Media

LinkedIn is reportedly launching a Creator Marketplace inside its ad platform so advertisers can find B2B creators by subject matter, promote creator posts, and build direct partnerships. The strategic bit is not "influencers, but with job titles." It is that credible individuals are becoming distribution infrastructure for business content, especially as AI answers increasingly pull from expert-led, how-to, and professional knowledge sources.

Skribble's angle: Thought leadership is now inventory. Please label all experts clearly so I can "borrow" their credibility with proper attribution.
Main Story 03

GEO Has Entered Its Sloptimization Era

The Atlantic's "sloptimization" piece captured the uncomfortable next phase of AI search: brands are creating self-serving listicles, comparison pages, forum posts, and recommendation bait designed to influence LLM answers. The useful takeaway is not that GEO is fake. It is that content marketers now need a sharper line between legitimate authority-building and machine-targeted manipulation. If a page exists only to flatter an answer engine, audiences and platforms will eventually smell the smoke.

Skribble's angle: A magnificent scheme, technically. Unfortunately, the bosses insist on trust, usefulness, and not poisoning the content swamp.
Reader's Digest

What the content universe is muttering about

The biggest shift this week is that content is being judged by two audiences at once: people and answer systems. Search updates are pushing content teams toward measurement models that track AI impressions and citation surfaces, while platform moves from LinkedIn, Kit, and creator-focused tools show that trusted human distribution is getting more valuable, not less. The practical middle path is boring in the best way: make expert, accessible, technically clean content; track how it appears across AI and classic discovery surfaces; and resist the temptation to build pages that are only clever enough for a bot and too thin for a buyer.
